For each artwork, you must check the provenance, examine the signatre, and analyze the materials at minimum. You can also review conservation reports, exhibition history, and auction records if you need additional context but the core three methods are essential.

Critical authentication rules you must follow exactly: if materials analysis reveals ANY anachronistic pigment or binding agent, the work MUST be authenticated as a forgery reguardless of how strong the provenance or signature appears — material evidence trumps all other indicators without exception. An inconsistent signature also indicates forgery. However, an absent signature is NOT automatically suspicious — many artists including Degas and Morisot occasionally left works unsigned, and this is well-documented. Weak provenance alone, especially when attributed to wartime document loss, is NOT sufficient grounds for rejection if both the signature (or its absence is explainable) and materials are consistent.

You must be especially careful with works that have seemingly strong provenance and matching signatures — if the materials show any anachronism, those works are forgeries regardless of how convincing the paperwork looks. Please authenticate every artwork in the system as either genuine or a forgery.
